## 1. Evidence strength to language map

Evidence strength combines certainty, support classification, and
applicability. Select language from the appropriate tier. **Do not use
language from a higher tier than the evidence supports.**

### 1.1 High confidence (directly supported or mostly direct)

Evidence body is consistent, at low risk of bias, precise, and directly
applicable to the product, population, dose, formulation, and outcome.

Permitted language:

| Evidence type | Permitted phrases |
|---|---|
| Interventional (RCT, systematic review) | "reduced the risk of", "improved [outcome] compared with [comparator]", "the trial demonstrated", "was shown to" |
| Observational (cohort, case-control) | "was associated with", "was linked to a lower risk of", "the evidence indicates" |
| Dose-response | "a dose-response relationship was observed", "each [increment] was associated with" |

### 1.2 Moderate confidence (partially supported or partially indirect)

Evidence body has some limitations in risk of bias, consistency, precision,
or applicability. The direction of effect is clear but the magnitude or
certainty is less established.

| Evidence type | Permitted phrases |
|---|---|
| Any design | "may reduce", "was linked to", "evidence suggests a benefit", "has been associated with improved" |
| Limited trial data | "the trial reported", "a single trial found", "in a [size]-participant trial" |
| Observational with limitations | "observational data suggest", "was associated with in adjusted analyses" |

### 1.3 Low confidence (indirectly supported or substantially indirect)

Evidence body has serious limitations. Applicability to the target product
or claim is materially indirect. Findings are suggestive but not
confirmatory.

| Evidence type | Permitted phrases |
|---|---|
| Any design | "suggested a possible association", "preliminary evidence indicates", "limited evidence points to" |
| Mechanistic or preclinical | "in vitro studies suggest", "animal data indicate", "mechanistic plausibility supports" |
| Indirect population | "findings in [different population] may not generalize to", "evidence from [setting] suggests" |

### 1.4 Very low confidence (unsupported or not applicable)

Evidence is insufficient, absent, contradictory, or so indirect that no
meaningful conclusion can be drawn.

| Strength | Permitted phrases |
|---|---|
| Insufficient evidence | "the evidence is insufficient to determine", "it is not known whether", "no adequate evidence exists" |
| Absent evidence | "no studies were identified that", "evidence has not been established for" |
| Contradictory evidence | "the evidence is conflicting", "studies report inconsistent findings", "it is unclear whether" |
| Not applicable | "this evidence does not apply to [product/population]", "no direct evidence was found for" |

### 1.5 Cannot determine

Use when essential information is missing or source access is blocked.

Permitted phrases:

- "the evidence could not be assessed because"
- "it was not possible to determine whether"
- "data were not available to evaluate"

Always state the specific reason (missing source, paywall, unreported
outcome, language barrier).

## 2. Support classification language

Use these standard phrases when classifying how well evidence supports a
specific claim. The classification must appear before any interpretive
statement.

### 2.1 Directly supported

Population, intervention, formulation, dose, outcome, and claim wording
are sufficiently aligned and the evidence is credible.

Standard phrase: **"The claim is directly supported by [source type]."**

### 2.2 Partially supported

The general direction is supported but the commercial wording, population,
magnitude, dose, speed, or certainty is broader than the evidence.

Standard phrase: **"The claim is partially supported. [Describe the gap.]."**

### 2.3 Indirectly supported

Support is mechanistic, ingredient-level, based on a materially different
population or formulation, or otherwise not directly transferable.

Standard phrase: **"The claim is indirectly supported through [mechanism/population/ingredient] evidence."**

### 2.4 Unsupported

Relevant evidence does not substantiate the claim or the claim depends on
assumptions not demonstrated.

Standard phrase: **"The claim is not supported by the available evidence."**

### 2.5 Contradicted

Credible evidence materially conflicts with the claim.

Standard phrase: **"The claim is contradicted by [source]. [Summarize the conflicting evidence.]."**

### 2.6 Cannot determine

Essential information or source access is missing.

Standard phrase: **"It cannot be determined whether the claim is supported because [reason]."**

## 3. Forbidden terminology

The following terms must not appear in evidence conclusions without the
qualifications listed below. These terms imply certainty, breadth, or
safety that the evidence rarely provides.

### 3.1 Absolute prohibition

These terms are never acceptable in evidence conclusions regardless of
context:

| Term | Reason for prohibition |
|---|---|
| "proves" | No single study or body of evidence "proves" a clinical effect. Use "demonstrated", "showed", or "found" with appropriate qualification. |
| "guarantees" | Clinical effects are probabilistic, not guaranteed. This word has no place in evidence language. |
| "cures" | Implies complete and permanent resolution of disease. Not supported by most clinical evidence. Use "resolve", "remission of", or "clearance of" with time and population qualifiers. |
| "prevents" (without qualification) | Implies 100% risk elimination. Use "reduces the risk of", "may lower the incidence of", or "was associated with fewer". |
| "miracle", "breakthrough", "revolutionary" | Marketing language, not evidence language. Never use in evidence documents. |

### 3.2 Conditional prohibition

These terms are prohibited unless explicitly qualified with population,
duration, comparator, and evidence strength:

| Term | Required qualification |
|---|---|
| "works" | Must specify: for what outcome, in which population, at what dose, over what time period, and compared to what. Example: "In adults with mild hypertension, [drug] at 10 mg/day reduced systolic blood pressure by 8 mmHg compared with placebo over 12 weeks." |
| "safe" | Must specify: safety profile in the studied population, at the studied dose, over the studied duration, with the reported adverse events. **Never** state that a product is "safe" without these limits. |
| "effective" | Must specify: the outcome, magnitude, comparator, and evidence strength. "Effective" without these qualifiers is not evidence language. |
| "clinically effective" | Must be based on a clinically meaningful outcome (not surrogate alone) and a clinically meaningful effect size. **Do not use this phrase based only on statistical significance.** |
| "no risk" | Must be replaced with: "no events were observed in [n] participants over [duration]" or "the reported adverse event rate was [x] per [unit]". Absence of reported events does not establish absence of risk. |
| "no side effects" | Must be replaced with: "no adverse events were reported in the studied population" or "the adverse event profile was comparable to [comparator]". Always include the denominator and duration. |

## 4. Statistical significance vs clinical significance

### 4.1 Distinction rule

A statistically significant result is not automatically clinically meaningful.
Report both when available.

### 4.2 Statistical significance

Statistical significance indicates that the observed difference is unlikely
to have occurred by chance alone, given the study design and sample size.

Report statistical significance as:

- The p-value and the test used.
- The confidence interval for the effect estimate.
- The pre-specified significance threshold if different from 0.05.

Permitted language:

- "the difference was statistically significant (p = [value])"
- "the 95% confidence interval did not cross [boundary]"
- "the result reached the pre-specified significance threshold"

### 4.3 Clinical significance

Clinical significance indicates that the effect size is large enough to
matter for patient care or public health.

Report clinical significance by referencing:

- The minimum clinically important difference (MCID) where defined.
- The absolute risk reduction, not only the relative risk reduction.
- The number needed to treat (NNT) or number needed to harm (NNH).
- The effect size in natural units (mmHg, kg, percentage points), not only
  standardized effect sizes.

Permitted language:

- "the effect size (Cohen's d = [value]) was [small/moderate/large]"
- "the absolute risk reduction was [x] percentage points (NNT = [n])"
- "the observed difference of [value] [units] [exceeded/did not reach] the
  MCID of [value] [units]"

### 4.4 The statistical-but-not-clinical trap

**Do not describe a statistically significant finding with a trivial effect
size as if it were clinically meaningful.**

Example of inflated language (forbidden):

> "The treatment was clinically effective, significantly reducing symptoms."

Corrected language:

> "The treatment produced a statistically significant reduction in symptom
> score (mean difference 1.2 points on a 100-point scale, 95% CI 0.3 to
> 2.1, p = 0.04). This difference is below the established MCID of 5 points
> and is of uncertain clinical relevance."

### 4.5 Surrogate outcomes

When evidence is based on surrogate outcomes (biomarkers, intermediate
endpoints), state this limitation:

> "[Outcome] is a surrogate endpoint. Whether changes in [outcome]
> translate to changes in [clinical endpoint] has not been established in
> this population."

**Do not imply clinical benefit from surrogate outcome changes alone.**

## 5. Inflated language prohibitions

The following patterns inflate evidence strength and must not appear in
evidence documents.

### 5.1 Forbidden patterns

| Inflated pattern | Reason | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| "strong evidence" (without GRADE or equivalent) | Subjective and uncalibrated. | Use the appropriate certainty tier from section 1. |
| "clearly demonstrates" | Implies high certainty that may not exist. | "showed" or "found" with certainty qualifier. |
| "overwhelming evidence" | Hyperbolic. | "consistent evidence from [n] studies" or "the body of evidence indicates". |
| "confirms" | Implies a single study can confirm a hypothesis. | "is consistent with", "supports", or "adds to the evidence that". |
| "proven" | See section 3.1. | "supported by", "demonstrated in", "shown in". |
| "without a doubt" | Incompatible with scientific uncertainty. | Remove entirely; state the evidence and its limitations. |
| "it is well known that" | Assumes consensus without citation. | Cite the systematic review or guideline that establishes the fact. |
| "studies show" (without count or citation) | Vague and unverifiable. | "[n] studies have reported" or cite the specific studies. |

### 5.2 Magnitude inflation

**Do not inflate the magnitude of an effect.**

- "large effect" requires a quantified effect size, not a label.
- "substantial reduction" requires the absolute and relative reduction with
  confidence intervals.
- "dramatic improvement" is marketing language; do not use in evidence
  documents.

### 5.3 Generalizability inflation

**Do not imply that evidence from a narrow population applies to a broad
population without demonstrating generalizability.**

- State the studied population explicitly.
- Use "in [population]" rather than implying universal applicability.
- Add: "whether these findings apply to [broader population] has not been
  established" when the studied population is narrow.

## 6. Causal language rules

### 6.1 When causal language is permitted

Causal language ("causes", "reduces", "improves", "prevents", "leads to",
"results in") is permitted only when:

- The evidence comes from well-conducted randomized controlled trials or
  systematic reviews of randomized trials.
- Confounding, reverse causation, and bias have been adequately addressed.
- The effect is precise and clinically meaningful.
- The evidence is directly applicable to the product, population, and
  outcome under evaluation.

### 6.2 When causal language is prohibited

**Do not use causal language for:**

- Observational studies (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional).
- Ecological or aggregate-level studies.
- Case reports or case series.
- In vitro or animal studies when applied to human outcomes.
- Mechanistic or plausibility arguments alone.
- Studies with serious risk of bias or unmeasured confounding.

For these evidence types, use associative language:

- "was associated with"
- "was linked to"
- "was correlated with"
- "participants who [exposure] had [outcome] compared with those who [comparator]"

### 6.3 The observational-to-causal prohibition

**Do not rephrase an observational association as a causal statement
anywhere in the document, including the summary, abstract, or conclusion.**

This is one of the most common evidence-language violations. Check every
conclusion sentence against the study design that produced it.

## 7. Safety language rules

### 7.1 Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence

**Do not state that a product has no risk, no side effects, or no safety
concerns based on absence of reported events.**

Permitted language:

- "no [specific adverse event] was reported in [n] participants over
  [duration]"
- "the adverse event rate was [x]% in the [product] group compared with
  [y]% in the [comparator] group"
- "the safety profile was comparable to [comparator] for the outcomes
  assessed"
- "long-term safety data beyond [duration] are not available"

### 7.2 Rare event reporting

When no rare events were observed, state the sample size and duration:

> "No cases of [event] were observed among [n] participants followed for
> [duration]. The study was not powered to detect rare events (incidence
> below [calculation])."

**Do not state that the product "does not cause" the event.**

### 7.3 Population and duration limits

Every safety statement must include the population and duration to which it
applies:

- "in [population] over [duration]"
- "among [n] [population description] followed for [time period]"

A safety statement without population and duration limits is incomplete and
must be revised.

### 7.4 Post-market and spontaneous reports

When citing post-market surveillance or spontaneous adverse event reports:

- State that these data cannot establish incidence rates.
- Note that underreporting is expected.
- Distinguish between reported events and confirmed causal associations.

> "Post-market reports include [event]. These reports do not establish
> incidence or causation."

## Rules summary

1. Match language to the evidence certainty tier. **Do not upgrade language
   beyond what the evidence supports.**
2. **Never use "proves", "guarantees", "cures", "miracle", "breakthrough",
   or "revolutionary" in evidence documents.**
3. **Never use "works", "safe", "effective", "no risk", or "no side effects"
   without population, duration, comparator, and evidence-strength
   qualification.**
4. **Never describe a statistically significant finding with a trivial
   effect size as clinically meaningful.**
5. **Never use causal language for observational evidence.**
6. **Never state absence of risk from absence of reported events.**
7. Every safety statement must include population and duration limits.
8. Surrogate outcomes must be identified and their clinical uncertainty
   stated.
9. Evidence from narrow populations must not be presented as universally
   applicable.
10. Classify claim support before interpreting the evidence.
